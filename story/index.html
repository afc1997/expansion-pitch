Title: Expansion
Written and Directed: Fisherman & Trout
Genre: Psychological Horror

Synopsis

After his estranged father's suicide, Jonathan Cameron inherits a destroyed rural mansion. Jon and his wife Esme move in to restore and sell it. But, their plan slowly unravels when Jon discovers a hidden hallway that shouldn't exist. As Jon delves deeper into the maze, he is attacked by masked intruders, haunted by visions of his past, and slowly alienated from everyone he trusts. In the film's climax, Jon's forced to navigate the heart of the labyrinth, where he must finally confront the pain he has spent his entire life avoiding.

Theme

There are things inside us we'd rather bury than face. Things we hide from ourselves, from our families, from the world. Those things don't disappear. They rot.

We want to make a movie about living inside a person who is consuming themselves because they're too afraid to look at what's hiding within them. We want it to feel like a nightmare, one constructed the way an architect might design a torture chamber. Claustrophobic, entrapping, filled with danger hiding behind the walls. We should feel almost like Jon's being surveilled. As if somehow, Richard is still watching over him, still pulling the strings.

CHARACTERS

Jonathan

Jon has spent his entire life running from his past. He's smart, loyal and secretly insecure. Jon deals with everything alone, inside his own head. But, when he inherits his father's house, Jon finds there's nowhere left to run. Our ideal actor is deeply charismatic, with a lot of anger underneath. Someone who projects intelligence and restraint while letting resentment leak through in small, dangerous ways. A performance that feels contained, until it isn't.

Esme

Esme has accepted what her life is going to look like. She will keep renting, she won't have the cash to start a family, she's going to spend the rest of her life with Jon. Esme is connected to her emotions, grounded while Jon goes flying into his mind. She believes everything can be fixed. But as she watches Jon deteriorate, her ability to withstand runs out. Our ideal actress is someone who can portray warmth and maternal care, but also feels like she's carrying a secret. Is that care genuine, or just a ploy to ruin Jon?

Parker

Parker is everything Jon wishes he could be. He fills a room effortlessly. He's charismatic, confident and physically imposing. He has a golden retriever energy that appears simply friendly, but underneath the facade, that energy might be something more calculated. Our ideal actor for Parker should be an endearing jock who toes the line between charming and threatening. As an audience, we should always be questioning his motive.

Richard

Richard Cameron hangs himself in the first scene of the movie, but his presence lingers long after. Throughout the film we see him in glimpses. He's brilliant, cruel, and the scariest thing in the world to Jon. To everyone else, he was a genius. To Jon, he was a monster. Our ideal actor is someone who can portray a larger than life figure, one filled with evil, magnetism and strength. Someone whose presence seeps into every frame, even when he's not in it.

The House

The house is an extension of Richard's mind. Monumental, isolated, fully wrecked. It has to cause shock when first seen. We're drawn to constructivism and parasitic architecture. Something that feels epic but also deeply wrong. We'll spend a lot of time within the house, so the corridors need to feel labyrinthine on their own. Throughout the seasons, the house will change. From destroyed, to in repair, to almost ready, and back to destroyed. The house is expressive of Jon's mental state. It's a character in its own right, one that's carrying a secret within its walls.

Unlike the house, the maze is always in pristine condition. Its walls are plaster white, incredibly tall, endlessly long.

The lighting is dim, showing just enough to make us feel there's something hiding. The hallways vary in size, narrowing and widening without logic. What's scariest is that we never know what's waiting behind a turn.

Visual Treatment

Tone

We're drawing from the look of Birth, The Shining, Rosemary's Baby, Funny Games, Safe, Taxi Driver. These are films that invite us into the internal minds of their heroes while presenting a bleak, observational, expressive view of an impossible situation. They ground their surrealism and convince us that these are real people simply dealing with something they can't explain. Our goal is to make the audience feel what Jon feels. Trapped, suspicious, unable to trust the people closest to them.

Camera

Our camera should feel mechanical. It's always controlled movement or completely static. It never moves handheld. We're isolating Jon from everyone around him, creating a me vs them atmosphere. We imagine a digital camera. No grain. Large depth of field. Infrared for night scenes. This is a movie that takes place largely at night, and we want the darkness to feel real. Like something's lurking in what can't be seen. Jealousy, distrust, fear. Our characters rarely speak their feelings, but they're incredibly present throughout the film. Our camera needs to find those moments and convey them to the audience without words.

Production Design

We want to lean into the jagged edges, the broken walls, the plastic tarps covering the furniture. This movie wants you to feel uneasy, and the production design of a house under construction should do exactly that. Mess, clutter, boxes, leaking roofs. Richard's presence lives in his objects, the frantic maze drawings, the rifle, the house itself.

Music

We want something abrasive and atonal, built from drones and dissonant strings that sit underneath every scene like something is wrong. The music should feel almost industrial. As if it were built from the sounds of construction.

Sound Design

We want the soundscape to have a big dynamic range. We keep things quiet so the loud moments feel violent. An impossibly quiet corridor… completely silent. Then a gunshot so loud it rings through the rest of the scene. When we enter the maze, the soundscape shifts entirely. The outside world disappears. What's left is Jon's breathing, his footsteps, and a liminal droning sound.

Edit

We take our time. Shots linger. Scenes breathe. The audience doesn't get the relief of a cut when they want one. We hold on faces too long. We let silence do the work. When we do cut, the cuts are hard. The edit mirrors Jon's psychology. Early on, the rhythm is steady. As Jon deteriorates, the cuts become disorienting.
